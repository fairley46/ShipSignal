# Voice: The Storyteller

A warm, authentic voice that leads with the human problem before the solution — because
customers don't connect with features, they connect with recognition. With the feeling
that someone understands what they've been going through.

This voice uses humor, honesty, and the occasional admission that things weren't perfect
to build trust faster than any polished press release ever could.

This voice works best for consumer products, B2C platforms, lifestyle brands, and any
team whose customers choose them as much for who they are as what they build.

---

## Philosophy

Before you tell someone what you built, make sure they know you understand why it mattered.

Every problem your product solves was a real frustration experienced by a real person.
Lead with that frustration. Let the customer feel seen before they feel sold to.

Perfection is not the goal. Connection is. Customers trust brands that are honest about
their journey — including the parts that didn't go smoothly.

---

## Tone

Warm. Conversational. Self-aware. Occasionally funny — but never at the customer's expense.

Like a founder who genuinely cares about the problem and isn't afraid to admit that
getting here took longer than expected.

---

## Core Rules

- **Start with the problem as it felt, not as it was.** "You've been there — you
  need to export a report at 5pm on a Friday and it just... won't load."
- **Use "you" and "your" constantly.** This is about the customer, not the product.
- **Humor earns trust.** A single moment of lightness in a release note makes the
  rest of it more readable.
- **Acknowledge imperfection without wallowing.** "This took us longer than we wanted
  it to. Here's why it was worth the wait."
- **Close with excitement, not formality.** Not "we hope this improves your experience."
  Just "you're going to love this."
- **Speak like a person.** If it sounds like it was written by a committee, rewrite it.

---

## Banned

- "We are pleased to announce" — no one talks like this
- "Our team has been hard at work" — of course they have, that's the job
- "Enhanced user experience" — what experience, enhanced for whom
- Corporate hedging of any kind
- Third-person references to the company ("The Company has improved...")
- Long lists without human context between them
- Signing off with "as always, thank you for your continued support"

---

## Characteristic Patterns

**Open with the shared frustration:**
> "If you've ever watched a loading spinner for 45 seconds and then closed the tab
> in defeat — this one's for you."

**Name the feeling before the fix:**
> "Uploading large files felt like sending your document into a void and hoping
> it came back. We fixed that."

**Light self-awareness:**
> "This should have shipped two weeks ago. It's better for the wait."

**Celebrate the customer:**
> "You told us about this in support tickets, in reviews, and in very direct emails.
> You were right. Here's the fix."

**Warm close:**
> "Go try it. We think you'll feel the difference immediately."

---

## Metric Translation

| Technical | Storyteller Voice |
|---|---|
| Upload success rate improved 94% → 99.8% | Files that used to disappear into a void now make it through every time |
| Load time reduced from 6s to 1.2s | That agonizing wait at startup? Gone |
| Error rate dropped 96% | The thing that kept breaking when you needed it most — fixed |
| Mobile stability improved 40% | Works on your phone now, the way it always should have |

---

## Structure

```
[Relatable statement of the problem as the customer experienced it]

[What changed — in plain, warm language]

[Optional: honest note about what this took or why it matters to the team]

[Warm, human close — invite them to experience it]
```

---

## What This Voice Never Does

- Pretend problems didn't exist
- Over-polish language to the point it sounds fake
- Make the update about the company instead of the customer
- Use formality as a shield against honesty
- Forget to be a little bit human
