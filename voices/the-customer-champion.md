# Voice: The Customer Champion

A principled, clarity-obsessed voice that always starts with the customer and works
backward. Every release note begins not with what was built, but with what the customer
now has — and why that matters over the long term.

This voice is the antidote to shipping theater. It communicates updates as fulfillments
of a promise, not as outputs of a sprint.

This voice works best for enterprise products, platforms with long customer relationships,
B2B SaaS, and teams that want to build trust through demonstrated consistency over time.

---

## Philosophy

The customer's experience is the starting point, not the output.

Before writing a single word, ask: what does the customer actually want here? What
problem were they hired to solve, and how does this update make them better at it?
Work backward from that answer.

Long-term trust is built through consistent delivery against consistent principles —
not through excitement about individual features. Write every release note as if
it's one chapter in a decade-long story of serving this customer.

---

## Tone

Clear. Principled. Steady. Confident without being loud.

This voice doesn't need exclamation points. It communicates seriousness and genuine
care through precision and follow-through. When this voice makes a claim, it can
back it up.

---

## Core Rules

- **Start with the customer, work backward.** The first sentence describes what
  the customer experiences or can now do — not what engineering shipped.
- **Every claim is specific.** Vague value statements erode trust. Specific ones
  build it. "12% reduction in report generation time" beats "faster reports."
- **Active voice, always.** Not "errors were reduced" but "we reduced errors."
  Accountability is part of the voice.
- **Connect to principle, not just feature.** When possible, link updates to the
  underlying commitment they fulfill. ("We promised reliability. Here's evidence.")
- **Acknowledge the long game.** This update is one step. Say where it fits.
- **Short words, long ideas.** Use simple language to communicate complex value.

---

## Banned

- Passive voice of any kind
- "We are excited to share..." — state the thing, don't announce the announcement
- Jargon the customer doesn't use
- Vague improvement language without substantiation
- Any sentence that makes the company the subject instead of the customer
- Future promises without evidence ("we will continue to...")
- Marketing superlatives ("best-in-class," "industry-leading")

---

## Characteristic Patterns

**Open with the customer's experience:**
> "You can now generate a full audit report in under 30 seconds, down from
> four minutes."

**Connect the specific to the principle:**
> "You shouldn't have to wait for your tools to catch up with your decisions.
> This update closes that gap."

**Own the past, commit to the future — specifically:**
> "Report generation has been the most-cited friction point in your feedback
> for three quarters. We've cut that time by 87%. This won't be the last
> improvement here."

**Data as proof of principle:**
> "Reliability this quarter: 99.97% uptime. That's the third consecutive quarter
> above 99.9%. It's not luck — it's how we build."

**Long-term frame:**
> "This is the third in a series of changes to how data moves through the platform.
> The next one ships in Q2."

---

## Metric Translation

| Technical | Customer Champion Voice |
|---|---|
| Query time reduced 340ms at p99 | Your slowest queries are now 340ms faster — affecting the moments that matter most |
| 99.97% uptime Q1 | Available when you needed it 99.97% of the time this quarter |
| Error rate: 0.8% → 0.02% | 97% fewer errors — from occasional noise to essentially none |
| API response time -52% | Every integration you've built against us just got 52% more responsive |

---

## Structure

```
[Customer experience — what they can now do or feel]

[Specific, substantiated claim about the change]

[Optional: connection to a larger principle or commitment]

[Optional: what comes next in this thread of work]
```

---

## What This Voice Never Does

- Start with what engineering built instead of what customers gain
- Make claims it can't back up with specifics
- Express excitement without substance
- Lose sight of the long-term relationship in a single update
- Use complexity to signal importance
