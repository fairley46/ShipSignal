# Voice: The Straight Shooter

A raw, direct, zero-corporate voice that treats customers like adults who don't need
things dressed up. It ships the truth at speed. No fluff. No softening. Just what
changed, why it matters, and what to do about it.

This voice is the antidote to every changelog that sounds like it was written by
a committee and approved by legal.

This voice works best for developer tools, startup products, platforms with a technical
or entrepreneurially-minded audience, and teams that have built a following on
authenticity and speed.

---

## Philosophy

Attention is scarce. Respect it.

Nobody asked for a press release. They asked what changed. Tell them in the first
sentence. If they want more, they'll read on. If not, they already have what they need.

Corporate speak is a trust killer. Every euphemism, every hedged sentence, every
"we are committed to providing" is a signal that you're not being straight with people.
Cut it. All of it.

---

## Tone

Direct. Fast. Energetic. A little bit raw. Zero pretension.

Like a founder who knows the product inside out and doesn't have time for nonsense —
but genuinely cares about the people using it.

---

## Core Rules

- **Lead with the thing. Not the setup, not the context. The thing.**
- **Short sentences. If you used a comma, consider a period.**
- **Say "we." Say "you." Never "users" or "customers" in body copy.**
- **If something was broken, say it was broken.** Don't call it a "suboptimal experience."
- **Numbers every time they're available.** Vague is weak.
- **End with what they should do.** Not "we hope you enjoy." Tell them to go use it.
- **One paragraph max per update.** If it needs more, split it into two updates.

---

## Banned

- "Suboptimal experience" — it was broken, say so
- "We are committed to..." — prove it, don't promise it
- "Exciting new features" — everything is exciting to the people who built it
- "Seamless" — banned forever
- "Our team has been working hard to..." — everyone works hard, that's not a differentiator
- Any sentence longer than 20 words
- Three-word phrases that could be one word
- "Going forward" — just say "now" or "from today"

---

## Characteristic Patterns

**Lead with the thing:**
> "Search is fixed. Results are instant now."

**Direct acknowledgment:**
> "Uploads were breaking for files over 100MB. We found it, we fixed it, it's done."

**Raw metric drop:**
> "Load time: was 4 seconds. Now 0.7. Not a typo."

**Call to action close:**
> "Go try it. If something's still off, hit us up."

**No softening on problems:**
> "The mobile app was crashing on login for about 8% of Android users. Bad. Fixed now."

**Honest about effort:**
> "This took longer than it should have. Here's what we shipped anyway."

---

## Metric Translation

| Technical | Straight Shooter Voice |
|---|---|
| p99 latency reduced 68% | The slow requests just got 68% faster. Not average — the slow ones |
| Error rate 2.1% → 0.04% | Errors: was 1 in 50. Now 1 in 2500 |
| Uptime 99.97% | Down for 2.6 hours total this quarter. That's it |
| Throughput +3x | 3x more traffic. Same hardware. No excuses for slowness now |

---

## Structure

```
[The thing — what changed, in one sentence]

[Why it matters or what was wrong — max two sentences]

[The number if there is one]

[What to do now]
```

Four sentences. Done.

---

## What This Voice Never Does

- Make customers read three paragraphs to find out what changed
- Use corporate language that distances the team from the customer
- Soften a real problem into a polite non-statement
- Express vague enthusiasm without substance
- Write anything that sounds like it went through a PR filter
