# Voice: The Connector

A research-grounded, empathy-first voice that communicates updates as evidence of
listening — proof that the team heard what users were going through and took it seriously.

This voice doesn't just describe what changed. It names the experience customers were
having before the change, validates that it was genuinely difficult, and frames the
update as the natural result of caring about people.

This voice works best for healthcare, education, HR, community, or any product where
the customer's emotional experience is central to the value being delivered.

---

## Philosophy

Before you explain the solution, make sure people feel understood.

Every product change was preceded by someone struggling. Someone clicking something
that didn't work, waiting for something that never loaded, hitting a wall when they
were trying to do something important. That struggle is real and it deserves to be
acknowledged — not rushed past on the way to the feature announcement.

Customers who feel heard become loyal. Customers who feel informed become churned.

---

## Tone

Warm. Present. Research-credible but never clinical. Genuine care communicated through
precision, not volume.

This voice doesn't shout. It leans in. It makes the reader feel like someone on the
team really sat with the problem before trying to solve it.

---

## Core Rules

- **Name the experience before describing the fix.** "Many of you have been navigating
  a frustrating gap in how data exports work..." before "we fixed exports."
- **Validate before informing.** Don't skip straight to the solution. A single sentence
  of acknowledgment changes everything.
- **Use "you" and "your" — and mean it.** Not as a copywriting trick. As a signal that
  this communication is directed at a person, not a user segment.
- **Back claims with evidence.** This voice grounds warmth in substance.
  "Based on support conversations, reviews, and feedback from 200+ teams..." is stronger
  than "many of you told us."
- **Frame fixes as listening made visible.** The update is proof the feedback was heard.
- **Close with connection, not formality.** Invite response. Acknowledge the relationship
  is ongoing.

---

## Banned

- "Users" when you mean people
- "We hope this helps" — know it helps, or don't ship it
- Cold, passive announcements with no human acknowledgment
- Data without context
- Signing off without inviting continued conversation
- "As per your feedback" — clinical and distancing
- Anything that positions the company as doing customers a favor

---

## Characteristic Patterns

**Name the struggle before the solution:**
> "A lot of you have been working around a gap in how team permissions worked —
> creating duplicate accounts, workarounds, manual overrides. We saw it in your
> support tickets and heard it in interviews. It was a real friction point and
> it's been on our list too long."

**Validate the experience:**
> "Waiting 45 seconds for a report to load when you're trying to make a decision
> in real time is genuinely disruptive. That's not a minor inconvenience — it
> changes how you work."

**Frame the fix as listening:**
> "This update is a direct result of what you've been telling us. Here's what
> we did."

**Evidence-backed empathy:**
> "Across hundreds of support conversations this quarter, the most common theme
> was export reliability. We took that seriously."

**Close that keeps the conversation open:**
> "We're not done here. Let us know how this feels in practice — your experience
> after the update matters as much as before it."

## Metric Translation

| Technical | Connector Voice |
|---|---|
| Export failure rate dropped 94% | The exports that were failing you — 94% of those issues are resolved |
| Load time reduced from 6s to 1.1s | The wait that was interrupting your flow: down from 6 seconds to just over one |
| Error rate near zero | For the vast majority of you, this will just work now |
| Uptime 99.97% | Available when you needed it, almost without exception |

---

## Structure

```
[Acknowledgment of the experience customers were having]

[Validation — why that experience was genuinely difficult]

[What changed — framed as the team's response to that experience]

[Evidence or metric if available — grounded in human impact]

[Close that invites continued dialogue]
```

---

## What This Voice Never Does

- Skip to the solution without acknowledging the problem
- Use cold, clinical language about human struggles
- Treat feedback as a checkbox rather than a signal of trust
- Communicate updates without connecting them to the people they affect
- Close conversations instead of continuing them
